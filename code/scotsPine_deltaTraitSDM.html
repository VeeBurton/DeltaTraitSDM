Applying the Î”TraitSDM to a multi-site provenance trial of native Scots pine (Pinus sylvestris)

This file documents the process of finding the most important climate variables which may be influencing native Scots pine height across Scotland. It shows the testing of a number of mixed-effects models, their comparison, and their implementation within a Î”TraitSDM framework to predict current and future distribution of the trait of interest.

Climate variables driving height

A number of strategies have been applied to explore the data and try to uncover the climate variables which may be explanatory for height.

According to pairplots:

  • MWMT_T
  • PAS_T
  • TD_T
  • DD0_T
  • MCMT_T
  • eFFP_T
  • Eref_T
  • EMT_T
  • DD_18_T
  • NFFD_T

According to PCA:

  • DD_18_T
  • FFP_T
  • bFFP_P
  • FFP_P
  • TD_P

According to exploration/difference between trial & provenance:

  • PAS
  • TD
  • MWMT
  • DD0
  • MCMT

So more generally, the factors driving height may be: degree days below 18, precipitation as snow, temperature extremes (warmest and coldest month temperatures, degree days below 0), frost free period and when this begins/ends, and temperature difference between coldest and warmest temperatures (continentality).

The data is nested Trial/Block/Provenance. Figure @ref(fig:boxplot) illustrates that there is variation in height between trials, and also within trials according to provenance. So there is value in fitting a mixed-effects model to explore height, with the random effects structure taking account of the different trials and provenances.

Î”TraitSDM

Several approaches were taken when applying the models within Î”TraitSDM. Firstly, climate surfaces for the â€˜climate normalâ€™ period (an average taken from 1960-1990) were used to estimate distribution. Secondly, separate climate surfaces were produced for the year when the seed was collected for the provenance trial (2007) and the year the seedlings were planted at the trial site (2012). Finally, a climate surface representing possible future climate change was used to estimate future distribution.
